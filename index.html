Research

I am a graduate student in quantitative psychology at McGill University, under the supervision of Dr. Jessica Flake. My research currently centers around (1) measurement equivalence in multi-group datasets, (2) the role of measurement in replication and original research, and (3) effects size measures in multilevel models.

You an find code for my past and current projects on the OSF and GitHub.

Outside of the lab, but still research-adjacent, I am interested in social determinants of health, income inequality, and universal basic income. I also have an affinity for great graphs.

Olympic Athlete Weight Distribution (#TidyTuesday 11/05/2019)

Being relatively new to data viz, for my first #TidyTuesday I decided to re-create a graph from r/dataisbeautiful of the height and weight distribution of Olympic athletes.

Original graph here. Code here.

This was a fun exercise for me; I did a few things for the first time, like using the viridis colour palette for the first time, and fiddling with the grid and axis labels on a graph. Big thanks to the original poster, u/takeasecond, for showing me their code as I worked on the final touches!

Additionally, it was satisfying to systematically re-create a close approximation after having worked through the first few chapters of R for Data Science by Garrett Grolemund and Hadley Wickham. I've improved since the beginning of the summer, when I used R for the first time to create a few bar graphs, hacking my way to a solution on a tight schedule.

Maps

With the holidays approaching, I stumbled on this great tutorial by Christian Burkhart for making maps, and decided to give it a try.

After making the basic maps, I used Coolors.co to generate and try colour palettes. I then used Photoshop to add a border and text (city, province/state, coordinates), and printed the maps at a local print shop. Et voila! Holiday gifts, and a learning opportunity.
